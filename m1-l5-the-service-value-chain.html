The Service Value Chain

The Service Value Chain is a concept within ITIL version 4 that suggests a means to organise how value is created by services within an organisation. The idea of a value chain likely originates within the Business Value Chain designed by Porter; however, Porter's model differentiates primary activities and secondary activities. Technology utilisation is a secondary delivery, and service comes at the end of the chain, following marketing and sales. Whereas service and technology are recognised, they are marginal. In the contemporary world, however, almost every business is highly reliant on information technology, and many organisations have shifted to becoming service providers rather than product retailers.

 

Service Value and Continuous Improvement

The following guide, a companion to the previous document, looks at the various stages of the Service Value Chain as represented above. Again, the methodology of value stream analysis is shared across organisations and platforms. ITIL® version 2 introduced an iterative approach, while version 3 relied heavily on the idea of lifecycles, foregrounding the processes of designing and decommissioning services. In ITIL® 4, however, focuses on the value stream, seeing service provision as a stage of interlocking steps. Bracketing the value chain are processes of engaging with service users to evaluate requirements and demand, and evaluating products and services delivered. Running in parallel each value chain is a process of continuous improvement and planning; continuous improvement looks at each stage, trying to optimise our activities and practices, while planning is also a process of documentation and forward thinking. Improvement and planning should not simply happen at the end of a cycle, but while a service is operational.
