Economic Impact of Digitisation

The digital economy is already having a seismic impact across the globe, yet the majority of the potential economic impact of digitisation has yet to be captured. The McKinsey Global Institute (MGI) estimated that an additional $13 trillion could be added to global GDP from 2018-2030 through digitisation, automation, and AI resulting from the creation of new business opportunities and the reinvestment of productivity gains into economies. 

Whilst the sectors with the highest levels of digitisation also demonstrate the the largest productivity growth, this period of digital transformation has yet to result in major productivity gains across the wider economy. It is an extraordinary fact that for the decade to 2017, the US witnessed its most sluggish productivity growth since the 1970s. Commentators have referred to this as ‘the productivity puzzle’, but one explanation is that the large productivity dividends to be realised from digitisation will not occur until digital technologies are more fully integrated into wider business workflow practices. MGI have estimated this may take to 2045 for full, worldwide diffusion of emerging smart automation and AI technologies in new use cases. A very significant factor influencing the rate of adoption will be the availability of skilled talent able to drive value from emerging technologies and this will be explored in greater depth later in the module.

MGI analysis of the United States, Europe, and China has shown that due to the complexities of business integration, even for well established digital technologies only a small amount of their potential has so far been realised. For traditional incumbent firms, digital channels accounted for just 26% of worldwide sales in 2018, only 31 percent of operations volume was digitally automated, and 25 percent of supply chain interactions digitised.

There is also high variability across sectors, with those focused on services or less physical products typically more advanced in their digitisation. This includes media and finance, with sectors such as pharmaceuticals and certain types of manufacturing lagging behind.

 

An Example from Australia

 

Australian Opportunities

These averages do not highlight geographic variations, with the US and China the most advanced. A study by Cisco and Gartner has placed Australia in the top 10 countries in the world for "digital readiness," but there is variation across states and as well as the US and China, it also sits behind countries including the UK, Switzerland, Sweden, Germany, the Netherlands. CSIRO has identified digitisation as a key global mega trend that Australia must embrace to remain economically competitive. One analysis has found that digital can represent the next frontier for Australian productivity and economic uplift, with the potential to add AU $140 billion to AU $250 billion to Australia’s GDP by 2025, based on currently-available technology alone.
