Edge Computing and IoT

 

Internet of Things

The so-called Internet of Things relates to the increasing amount of internet-connected devices; these can include sensors, video devices, drones, robotic equipment or household appliances. Often, the devices will not have significant memory or processing power themselves, and so they will rely on external resources, such as cloud services, to support their operation. The below video documents some of the work being undertaken at Northeastern's Institute for the Wireless Internet of Things, a research institute in Boston and Burlington: 

Cloud services may simply store the data, or collect and perform analysis on metrics coming from the devices. At the same time, we are now seeing more use of feedback, where the services on the cloud will guide the operation of the device itself. This is particularly prominent in home assistant devices, such as Google Home or Amazon Alexa, where the device will transmit and process verbal requests to return information. Increasingly we are seeing the devices used to provide information and even guidance to autonomous vehicles. The latency involved in sending and receiving information from a data centre will often raise issues. To get around this problem, developers will make use of so-called edge locations. These locations were originally set up by cloud providers to cache data from cloud storage, but have since been upgraded to allow for processing of data. Edge Computing and the Internet of Things are often considered part of the movement towards Ubiquitous Computing. Ubiquitous Computing implies a world where computers are seamlessly integrated with daily life.

Ubiquitous Computing

Ubiquitous Computing implies that computers are everywhere and nowhere. They are a part of all of our daily processes from the work to the home, but they are not visible or noticeable because the user experience has been developed to the point where we barely notice that we are interacting with intelligent devices. These devices have the potential to collect huge amounts of data, and by analysing this data we have the potential to improve people's working and home lives dramatically. At the same time, the risks of a digital divide or mass surveillance also need to be considered.
