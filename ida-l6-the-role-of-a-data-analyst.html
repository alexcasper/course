The Role of a Data Analyst

Without meaningful analysis, data is just disorganised information. 

Newman (2020) posits that ‘analytics is probably the most important tool a company has today to gain customer insights. This is why the Big Data space is set to reach over [US]$273 Billion by 2023 and companies like Microsoft, Amazon and Google among so many others are so heavily invested in not only collecting data but enabling data for the enterprise.’ 

This outcome can be achieved only with the expertise of data analysts.

Across the board, companies report that finding the right talent is their biggest hurdle when trying to integrate data and analytics in their existing operations. In a recent McKinsey & Company survey, approximately half of executives across geographies and industries reported greater difficulty recruiting analytical talent than filling any other kind of role. Forty percent say retention is also an issue (Brown & Gottlieb, 2016).

Data Analysts Skill Set

Data analysts leverage traditional mathematical theory and statistical methods as well as machine learning and AI to interpret data and present it in a sophisticated, clean and unbiased manner.

The U.K.’s Government Digital Service has published guidance on these skills and highlighted how they are applied for different levels of seniority.

Guidance: Data Analyst

You can see that the role of the data analyst is varied and complex, marrying:

  • traditional academic arithmetic,
  • emerging technologies and
  • future-focused innovation.

As well as having skills in statistical interpretation, the data analyst must be a skilled communicator who can derive meaning from the data and present a coherent narrative to a range of stakeholders and for a variety of purposes.

Henke et al. (2016) conclude, ‘as data grows more complex, distilling it and bringing it to life through visualization is becoming critical to help make the results of data analyses digestible for decision makers’.
