 Knowledge Organisation

Seen simply, knowledge generation process can be considered to be the following:

Effectively, we take data, and perform one or many transformations on it, and use the result to support our organisational goals. This information is effectively data that has been made more useful - through cleaning, shaping, blending, modelling or visualisation. We speak of data in terms of lifecycles and pipelines.

 Chalkley, T. Illustration accompanying Cleveland, H. 1982. 'Information as Resource' in The Futurist. 12. December. 

The above cartoon and associated article is one of the key inspirations for the concept of the Data-Information-Knowledge-Wisdom pyramid. This concept expands on the idea of transforming data through various stages of enrichment towards wisdom. This image invokes a number of things - the idea of an ascent towards enlightenment, or an intellectual hierarchy. At the same time, the reality of data analysis is such that a huge amount of data is now needed to produce effective insights. 

Think about the data collected by your organisation - how much of it is transformed into effective insights, and how much of it languishes unutilised. Similarly, who has access to the important 'big-picture' insights? The hierarchal nature of many large organisations as well as the limited resources for data analysis and visualisation meant that the pyramid became a popular representation. At the same time, it is not the only possible representation of knowledge. If you look at the original comic, the final panel shows that the individual is able to see a much bigger picture, including the relationships between entities.
