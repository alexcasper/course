Succeeding as a Data-Driven Organisation

To succeed as a data-driven organisation, data analytic practice must be embedded across the organisation. If data analysis is confined to specialised areas of the business, the overall gain from data practice will be average.

When discussing leadership in analytics, Deloitte (2017) describe a luxury retailer.

. . . the CEO took a top-down approach to ensure all his employees – from store managers to merchandisers and back office designers – had the tools they needed to adopt a data-driven mindset. This began with the recognition that insights are required to drive the customer experience and deal with real-world business concerns – not to resolve data issues. ‘The insight-driven approach caused us to understand our customers in different ways to create different labels’ he notes. ‘We were able to take this information and adapt it to talk to both existing customer segments and entirely different customers.’ (p. 13)

— Deloitte (2017)

Organisations are harnessing data analytics to fuel innovation. In their report for the McKinsey Global Institute, Henke et al. (2016) highlight some examples:

  • Because a few milliseconds can translate into additional millions of dollars in revenue, Google is running a vast number of experiments to induce faster search queries.
  • Netflix has refined its recommendation engine and rolled it out to global customers – and a study released by the company estimates that it brings in US$1 billion in annual revenue.
  • American courier giant UPS feeds data into its ORION platform to dynamically determine the most efficient routes for its drivers.

Data Analytics and Business Models

There is a disparity between digitally native organisations and those that have a more traditional business model. While the digitally native organisations are ‘built for data analytics-based disruption from their inception’, most legacy companies ‘remain in the starting gate’.

In the eyes of Henke et al. (2016), ‘There are now major disparities in performance between a small group of technology leaders and the average company – in some cases creating winner-take-most dynamics. Leaders such as Apple, Alphabet/Google, Amazon, Facebook, Microsoft, GE, and Alibaba Group have established themselves as some of the most valuable companies in the world’.

The same trend can be seen among privately held companies. The leading global unicorns have been leveraging data from the start, whereas other organisations ‘have invested in data and analytics technology but have yet to realise the payoff, while others are still wrestling with how to take the initial steps’.

Additional challenges arise because grasping the data is only part of the modern, data-driven organisation. In a Deloitte report surveying the future of data analysis and the shifts occurring in the landscape as far back as 2014, Cheng, Lewin, Nugent, and Hillard (2014) confirmed that ‘firms are recognising that they need a centralised policy approach to extract value from data – big or otherwise’.

Leaders recognise that business models must evolve to manage and benefit from the emergence and evolution of data analytics. For example, the Australian Government has adopted a whole-of-government approach to big data by establishing a multi-agency working group and a Data Analytics Centre of Excellence (DACoE) to focus on the development of capabilities.
