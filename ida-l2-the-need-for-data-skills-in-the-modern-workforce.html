The Need for Data Skills in the Modern Workforce

Deloitte Access Economics (2018) forecasts that the Australian data science workforce will grow from 301,000 people in 2016–17, to 339,000 people in 2021–22. The average annual growth rate of 2.4% is stronger than the 1.5% per annum growth that is forecast for the entire Australian labour force.

Although this growth is notable, the growth in demand for data-related skills is much more significant. Our future workforce won’t all be data scientists, but a large percentage of them will need to be able to manage and analyse data to do their jobs in finance, marketing, healthcare, education and most other professions. This is illustrated in an IBM and Burning Glass report (Markow, Braganza, & Taska, 2017) into the American economy that noted that the number of job postings specifically for data scientists (+5%) did increase in 2016, but there was a dramatic growth in job descriptions requesting data-centred skills.

For example:

  • Clinical Data Analysis: +54%
  • Data Science: +40%
  • Quantitative Data Analysis: +38%
  • Data Visualisation: +31%
  • Data Engineering: +28%
  • A/B Testing: +22%
  • Machine Learning: +17%

This diversity points to a new reality: success in the jobs of the future will depend on essential ‘transcendent skills’ – such as data analysis – that are valuable in multiple roles and contexts.

As data analysis becomes central to multiple roles and functions, it can no longer be thought of as a purely quantitative capability. Becoming a data-driven organisation requires three reinforcing strategic pillars:

  1. The ability to identify and manage multiple sources of data.
  2. The analytical capability to garner information and insights from that data.
  3. The organisational capability to transform those insights into effective decision-making that results in organisational value.

 

Fundamental Data Analytics Skills

Of course, other highly complex data-driven functions that are within the scope of data scientists, but in the age of analytics opportunities abound for professionals with fundamental data analytics skills.

Critical Thinking and Problem Solving

As computers become more powerful and algorithms become more sophisticated, software is increasingly used to perform traditional data-analyst tasks. This trend towards more use of computers is exacerbated by the growing adoption of artificial intelligence and machine-learning approaches, and makes the traditional human ‘soft skills’ of critical thinking and problem-solving even more important. The human role of ‘computational thinking’ will increasingly be used to creatively translate organisational challenges into questions that can then be solved by data. To set hypotheses, run experiments, and draw conclusions, humans must also be appropriately sceptical, recognise bias and reject conclusions that do not stand proper scrutiny.

Statistics for Data Analysis

Computers are completing increasingly complex mathematical calculations and, although you might not need a maths degree to complete data analysis, those working with data do need to have acute quantitative skills. These skills include a broad understanding of the models that are being run by software, and engagement with how mathematical models can be employed to solve real-world challenges. You also need a sound understanding of the fundamentals of statistics and will use spreadsheet software such as Excel to manipulate data and run statistical models.

Data Management

To be valuable, the vast amounts of data produced by an organisation must be organised and structured so it can be analysed to draw conclusions. It is therefore essential to be able to use databases and other technologies to collect, organise and manipulate large amounts of data. Structure Query Language (SQL) is a highly effective, accessible tool that enables analysts to create powerful queries to retrieve specific information from complex databases.

Data Communication, Visualisation and Storytelling

Many organisations collect and analyse data effectively but are unable to transform their insights into effective decision-making that results in organisational value. They fall at the final hurdle because they can’t present data in an accessible form, or communicate their findings in a way that has meaning for broader stakeholders. Increasingly, organisations are creating data visualisations that bring insights to life, and using them to tell stories that show how the analysis is relevant to the decision. This is increasingly done through business intelligence (BI) platforms. Tableau is the market leader in this field.

Ethics

Because we rely increasingly on computer calculations to make decisions, we need to ask the right questions and provide the right inputs in an ethical way. Computer algorithms are created by humans; they can therefore incorporate our conscious and unconscious biases. We need to be aware of this danger and put in place governance structures to address it.
